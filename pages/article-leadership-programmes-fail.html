Leadership Development

Why Most Leadership Programmes Fail — And What the Good Ones Do Differently

GB
Ganesh Balasubramanian · 8 min read
← Back to Insights

We've observed hundreds of leadership programmes across organizations — as participants, as facilitators, and as consultants brought in to fix what went wrong. The pattern is remarkably consistent: most fail not because the content is bad, but because the diagnosis never happened.

A typical scenario: an HR team decides the organization "needs leadership training." They shortlist a few popular frameworks, pick whichever sounds most credible, and roll it out to a cohort of managers. Six months later, engagement scores haven't moved, the same conflicts persist, and the only lasting impact is a binder nobody opens again.

The Diagnosis Comes First

Every leadership intervention should start with a simple but rigorous question: what specifically is broken, for whom, and why? Not "we need better communication" — but "our product and engineering leads talk past each other in planning meetings, and it's costing us two sprints a quarter."

This sounds obvious. In practice, it's the step almost everyone skips. Diagnosis takes time, requires uncomfortable conversations, and doesn't produce a tidy deliverable the way a training calendar does. So organizations default to the prescription without ever confirming the diagnosis.

"The most expensive leadership programmes we've seen were also the least diagnosed. Money spent without diagnosis isn't investment — it's a guess with a bigger budget."

What the Good Ones Do Differently

The leadership programmes that actually shift behaviour share a few common traits:

  • They start with listening, not content. Structured interviews, 360 feedback, or simple skip-level conversations reveal what's actually happening — not what people assume is happening.
  • They name the real problem specifically. "Improve communication" becomes "give and receive difficult feedback without it becoming personal."
  • They build in follow-through. A single workshop changes nothing on its own. The interventions that work include spaced reinforcement — coaching check-ins, peer accountability, or manager-led follow-ups weeks after the initial session.
  • They measure behaviour, not satisfaction. A great feedback score on the workshop itself tells you people enjoyed the day. It tells you nothing about whether they're leading differently three months later.

A Practical Starting Point

If you're considering a leadership development investment, before you select a programme, spend two weeks doing nothing but listening. Talk to the leaders themselves, their peers, and their direct reports. Ask what specifically gets in the way of better leadership in your context — not in general, but in your organization, this year, with these people.

That diagnosis will tell you more about what programme you actually need than any vendor brochure will.
